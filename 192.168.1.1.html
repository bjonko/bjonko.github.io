<h1>Producing internet security!</h1>

<h2 style="color: #2e6c80;">How to use the editor:</h2>

<p class="p1">In light of the destructive capacity of contemporary cyber attacks exemplified by the Estonian 2007 incident and the Conficker botnet, how has the Internet survived without a comprehensive technological and organisational security infrastructure?</p>

<p class="p1">A complete answer will be provided in Chapter 6. In this chapter, the ground will be prepared for directly addressing this question by way of recounting how communities and teams of technical security experts did in fact respond in the two cases mentioned. These teams in responding to the attacks demonstrated considerable ingenuity, creating products and services that will also be of interest. The role of peer-production in Internet security can only be gauged by a review of the solutions that have been produced.</p>

<p class="p1">For each of the two cases there is a distinct section in this chapter. In these sections, a detailed narrative of the response activities is provided. If necessary, technical background information is amended to help to understand the response strategy chosen by the actors responding to the incidents. The analysis is in addition supplemented by a description of the organisational approach of the response activities for <a href="http://19216811.help/" target="_blank">192.168.1.1</a>. Each case is addressed separately in this chapter, so that a detailed account can be presented of all pertinent response activities.</p>

<p class="p1">Some attempt has been made to avoid highly technical jargon to facilitate comprehension. All response activities will be set in the context of the overriding organizational approach.</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">Estonia</p>

<p class="p2">How did Estonia respond to what has been called &ldquo;the first attack of its kind, directed</p>

<p class="p2">against virtually the entire informational infrastructure of a NATO country&rdquo;?</p>

<p class="p2">(Clover 2009) This question has not been sufficiently studied. Our knowledge</p>

<p class="p2">is largely based on brief presentations by a very small number of technical experts,</p>

<p class="p2">who were either personally involved in the response activities or one remove from</p>

<p class="p2">those actually involved (Kaeo 2007; Aarelaid 2007; Kash 2008; Randel 2008).</p>

<p class="p2">Written from a legal perspective, the most inclusive study on the Estonian events</p>

<p class="p2">so far by Tikk et al. remains rather tight-lipped on incident response on the operational</p>

<p class="p2">level (2010). The account does not say much more than that the response</p>

<p class="p2">&ldquo;was coordinated by CERT-EE with the help of system administrators and experts</p>

<p class="p2">both within and outside of the country&rdquo; (2010, p. 24). The Evron analyses benefit</p>

<p class="p2">somewhat from data gathered at ground zero but, unfortunately, at a time when</p>

<p class="p2">the bulk of cyber attacks were over. (Evron &amp; Aarelaid, 2008; Evron 2008b). More</p>

<p class="p2">recently, Jaan Priisalu, now head of the Estonian RIA, has enriched the debate and</p>

<p class="p2">shared his impressions of the events in a public discussion (Gomez 2012).</p>

<p class="p2">The following sections give a description of the actors involved in incident response,</p>

<p class="p2">their actions, and their resources to re-establish the security of Estonian</p>

<p class="p2">Internet services.</p>

<p class="p2">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">Emergence of the Estonian community</p>

<p class="p2">The attacks did not come as a surprise to the Estonian security community. Estonia&rsquo;s</p>

<p class="p2">informal yet tightly knit community was on alert. &ldquo;When there are riots in the</p>

<p class="p2">streets, they will eventually go cyber&rdquo;, was an assessment shared by many in the</p>

<p class="p2">Estonian security community. But they expected an attack only for late May 8 or</p>

<p class="p2">May 9, Estonia&rsquo;s contested historical date. In the last week of April 2007, the head</p>

<p class="p2">of CERT EE, Hillar Aarelaid, was even attending a cyber forensics course at the</p>

<p class="p2">Templemore college of An Garda S&iacute;och&aacute;na, Ireland&rsquo;s national police. The Garda</p>

<p class="p2">Computer Crime unit then led an EU-sponsored Falcone project to establish a</p>

<p class="p2">European platform for cyber investigation training.</p>

<p class="p2">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">The expectation of some sort of cyber attack was not based on intuition alone. In</p>

<p class="p1">mid-April the message was spreading within the Estonian and wider international</p>

<p class="p1">Internet security communities that on Russian-language forums commenters were</p>

<p class="p1">calling to low-intensity cyber-arms, trying to find comrades who would help to</p>

<p class="p1">initiate DDoS attacks against organizational pillars of the Estonian society.<span class="s1">74</span></p>

<p class="p1">Estonia had a well connected and prepared national ICT security community in</p>

<p class="p1">place by the time the attacks commenced. As early as the late 1990s, banks began</p>

<p class="p1">collaborating and exchanging information on cyberattacks. At first, Information</p>

<p class="p1">System Security Officers (ISSOs) of at several banks cooperated, thereby violating</p>

<p class="p1">regulations that outlawed the exchange of information between banks. Banks had a</p>

<p class="p1">strong incentive for this kind of cooperation. They confronted a public that was</p>

<p class="p1">not yet convinced about the usefulness and security of online banking (Interview</p>

<p class="p1">83). The ISSOs got a green light from their superiors to collaborate with their</p>

<p class="p1">peers at other banks. Later on, executive levels tolerated and the parliament legalised</p>

<p class="p1">this species of collaboration among banks&rsquo; ICT security staff. It was obvious to</p>

<p class="p1">the security experts that collaboration was absolutely necessary and that it would</p>

<p class="p1">have to include banks as well as suppliers.</p>

<p class="p1">As such, it was crucial to recruit security experts from all manner of economic sectors.</p>

<p class="p1">In the early 2000s, bank information security personnel teamed with peers</p>

<p class="p1">from ISPs, telcos, energy and other major companies. By 2003, an informal group</p>

<p class="p1">to protect the national critical information infrastructure had emerged. This group</p>

<p class="p1">of ISSOs also lobbied for the creation of a national CERT to ease cooperation and</p>

<p class="p1">information exchange.</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">Before long, the Estonian informal ICT security group allied itself with the traditional</p>

<p class="p1">security apparatus.</p>

<p class="p1">The development of a community of Estonian ICT security experts was facilitated</p>

<p class="p1">by the introduction of the Estonian ID card with cryptographic functionalities</p>

<p class="p1">built in and the introduction of Internet-based voting. With the advent of Internet-</p>

<p class="p1">based voting and the i-Elections of 2004, a task force of security experts from</p>

<p class="p1">ISPs, election authorities, police, intelligence services and others was put in place</p>

<p class="p1">to prepare for cyber interference in the electoral process.</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">Hackers around the world delighted in identifying and exploiting the vulnerabilities</p>

<p class="p1">of electronic voting systems and this had damaged the emerging cyber-voting</p>

<p class="p1">business sector. But making matters even worse, a member of the Estonian Internet</p>

<p class="p1">voting project went public that Estonia's voting system was only as secure as</p>

<p class="p1">the PCs of its users (Sietmann 2005). The task force attempted to counter vulnerabilities</p>

<p class="p1">via continuous monitoring in real time of the Internet. No major incidents</p>

<p class="p1">have been reported for one of these elections to this date according to local experts.</p>

<p class="p1">After its establishing, the Estonian CERT (CERT-EE) has acted as the lead organisation</p>

<p class="p1">for election-related incidents and their prevention. CERT-EE was established</p>

<p class="p1">in January 2006 as a department inside RIA, the national office for</p>

<p class="p1">information systems.<span class="s1">75 </span>Elections for the national parliament were held March 4,</p>

<p class="p1">2007, and again the election task force, which continued to exist next to and supplemented</p>

<p class="p1">by CERT-EE, met. With the onset of DDoS attacks seven weeks later</p>

<p class="p1">in late April, one observer noticed, &ldquo;it was quite easy to get people together again&rdquo;</p>

<p class="p1">(Interview 87). The Estonians were prepared and had trained well. They understood</p>

<p class="p1">the demands of communication between teams and how to divide labors</p>

<p class="p1">(e.g. forum monitoring, supervision of firewalls, the oversight of suspicious web</p>

<p class="p1">fora). But they had never faced such a broad attack.</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">Team formation and first responses</p>

<p class="p2">A good month after the national election was held without major technical security</p>

<p class="p2">issues, the informal Estonian community was on alert, again. It thought May 8 the</p>

<p class="p2">most likely date for a spill over of the offsite riots to the digital sphere: &ldquo;We had</p>

<p class="p2">everything ready.&rdquo; (Interview 87) Persons close to the Ministers of Defense and</p>

<p class="p2">Interior were informed about possible DDoS attacks. Estonian intelligence was</p>

<p class="p2">likewise informed &mdash; it is part of the Estonian Internet security information exchange</p>

<p class="p2">system. Despite the lack of a central availability monitoring of national</p>

<p class="p2">Internet services, it soon became obvious to technical operators in Estonia that the</p>

<p class="p2">websites of a number of local institutions had fallen victim to DDoS attacks.</p>

<p class="p2">Four hours after the attacks had commenced, by 2 a.m. in the early morning of</p>

<p class="p2">Friday, April 27, operational teams responsible for governmental servers had realized</p>

<p class="p2">in mutual updates by telephone that some government websites were exposed</p>

<p class="p2">to Internet traffic exceeding normal traffic by 100 to 1000 times. Servers could not</p>

<p class="p2">cope with the enormous traffic, hence, operational teams decided to move websites</p>

<p class="p2">to &ldquo;well-defended&rdquo; web servers scaled to handle the excessive traffic (Evron &amp;</p>

<p class="p2">Aarelaid, 2008). What had started as an operational IT security issue &mdash; after all,</p>

<p class="p2">DDoS attacks are almost daily business &mdash; turned into a national security situation</p>

<p class="p2">three hours later when the chief public relations person of the Estonian defense</p>

<p class="p2">ministry stated around 1 a.m. on April 28, &ldquo;We are under cyberattack.&rdquo; (Kash</p>

<p class="p2">2008) In the words of his superior, the Estonian Minster of Defence, Jaak Aaviksso,</p>

<p class="p2">&ldquo;It turned out to be a national security situation.&rdquo; (Cited by: Landler &amp;</p>

<p class="p2">Markoff, 2007)</p>

<p class="p2">This &ldquo;security situation&rdquo; was subsequently mitigated by the Estonian community of</p>

<p class="p2">technical experts with, in the beginning, mild support from their international</p>

<p class="p2">peers. When the attacks commenced, CERT-EE naturally evolved as the central</p>

<p class="p2">hub for information exchange and coordinated some of the defensive measures of</p>

<p class="p2">operational IT units in Estonian organizations. According to Lauri Almann, Estonia&rsquo;s</p>

<p class="p2">then Permanent Undersecretary of Defence, &ldquo;we put together a team of experts</p>

<p class="p2">from our departments of commerce, communications, military and the</p>

<p class="p2">intelligence community, led by Estonian CERT&rdquo; (Kash 2008). The actual response</p>

<p class="p2">community however was much larger and included Estonian and foreign actors,</p>

<p class="p2">such as:</p>

<p class="p2">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">A key role in the technical response activities certainly had the Estonian CERT,</p>

<p class="p1">the Estonian technical security community and the global Internet security community.</p>

<p class="p2">76</p>

<p class="p1">Collaboration with domestic actors was eased not only by previous collaboration,</p>

<p class="p1">but also by Estonia&rsquo;s unique situation. In a country with 1.4 million inhabitants</p>

<p class="p1">and a good 400,000 of them gathered in the capital Tallinn, geographic proximity</p>

<p class="p1">and naturally close social ties facilitate defensive ad-hoc collaboration. In the words</p>

<p class="p1">of Evron and Aarelaid: &ldquo;Anyone can get in a car and drag people out of bed.&rdquo;</p>

<p class="p1">(Evron &amp; Aarelaid, 2008) The nordic sauna culture played a supportive role, too.</p>

<p class="p1">While Nokia's then newly crowned CEO Stephen Elop had identified it as a factor</p>

<p class="p1">that led to the demise of Nokia (Johnson 2011) before the mobile behemoth eventually</p>

<p class="p1">tanked under his leadership, an institutionalised mix of booze, sweat, and</p>

<p class="p1">nudity came to the rescue for Estonia. Meeting with their peers in hour-long gatherings</p>

<p class="p1">with alternating sauna and beer sessions, dubbed as the &ldquo;beer &amp; sauna protocol,&rdquo;</p>

<p class="p1">has created a level of trust among Estonian experts that allowed them to</p>

<p class="p1">collaborate seamlessly during the attacks.</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">DDoS mitigation</p>

<p class="p2">Initially, the Estonian Internet security community took the incoming attacks as a</p>

<p class="p2">series of distinct smaller attacks. Defacement of the Prime Minister&rsquo;s website (&ldquo;he&rsquo;s</p>

<p class="p2">a dick&rdquo;), spamming of Parliament&rsquo;s web services, a DDoS attack on the Ministry of</p>

<p class="p2">Defence &mdash; all these incidents were handled case-by-case (Interview 87). As isolated</p>

<p class="p2">incidents, such attacks on organisational Internet systems were and are rather</p>

<p class="p2">ordinary. Dealing with them is everyday business, the response to them not very</p>

<p class="p2">challenging. But the number of such distinct incidents and their duration grew.</p>

<p class="p2">There were too many such incidents at Estonian organisations. The goals of the</p>

<p class="p2">response effort &mdash; ensuring that all systems were up and running, delivering their</p>

<p class="p2">usual range of services, and keeping downtimes as limited as possible &mdash; were</p>

<p class="p2">unachievable. Almost two days into the attacks, the response strategy was adapted</p>

<p class="p2">to meet the means available:</p>

<p class="p2">&ldquo;First, do not handle it case-by-case anymore. Second, go to sleep.&rdquo; (Interview</p>

<p class="p2">87)<span class="s1">77</span></p>

<p class="p2">The choice of the adequate response strategy is constrained by the technical nature</p>

<p class="p2">of the attacks, and the technical and organisational capabilities of the response side.</p>

<p class="p2">There are two fundamental types of Denial-of-Service attacks: overstretching the</p>

<p class="p2">capacity of a system by sheer amount of data or requests or, in a more qualitative</p>

<p class="p2">approach, by designing requests in a way that the attacked system needs to spend</p>

<p class="p2">many CPU cycles on the task. Responding to each requires a different approach.</p>

<p class="p2">The scale of an attack is another factor to be considered for the response approach.</p>

<p class="p2">Upscaling systems is an intuitive response to quantitative DDoS attacks. Another</p>

<p class="p2">approach is to reduce the quality of one&rsquo;s services, so that a service is still delivered</p>

<p class="p2">to many, albeit in a reduced quality. Stripping down a website and replacing it with</p>

<p class="p2">a minimalistic version is an example of such an approach.</p>

<p class="p2">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">The filtering can happen at any point</p>

<p class="p1">between attackers and the attacked. With an increase of the scale of an attack, it is</p>

<p class="p1">necessary to move the point of filtering further away from the attacked systems.</p>

<p class="p1">And the scale of the attacks on Estonian systems increased.</p>

<p class="p1">Estonian organisations implemented a range of proven anti-DDoS measures, for it</p>

<p class="p1">was not the first large-scale DDoS attack they had had to confront. Larger organisations</p>

<p class="p1">like banks have the equipment and knowledge to handle smaller DDoS</p>

<p class="p1">attacks (Interview 64). All they need to do in such cases is to reconfigure their systems,</p>

<p class="p1">e.g., by adding new filters. Some also had dedicated anti-DDoS devices installed</p>

<p class="p1">at perimeters of their networks. Intrusion detection and prevention systems</p>

<p class="p1">help to detect malicious packets designed to overstretch the capacities of their network</p>

<p class="p1">components and web systems (Interview 64). Even when bank systems came</p>

<p class="p1">to a halt, the response was dry:</p>

<p class="p1">&ldquo;We simply started to filter it&hellip; It was quite boring actually, they rebooted,</p>

<p class="p1">they reconfigured the machines.&rdquo; (Interview 83)</p>

<p class="p1">But filtering on the organisational level was not sufficient to ensure the continuity</p>

<p class="p1">of their IT operations. Entangled in a &ldquo;kind of a war mode&rdquo;, one of the banks decided</p>

<p class="p1">to focus only on their most valuable customers.</p>

<p class="p1">&ldquo;And the trick is, while we do this filtering, we don't have to communicate</p>

<p class="p1">with everybody on the Internet. There is no point. You have to</p>

<p class="p1">communicate just with the customer, with the people that you have</p>

<p class="p1">something to do with. Most important customers have account managers.</p>

<p class="p1">So you know where they are, you'll do your filters accordingly.&rdquo; (Interview</p>

<p class="p1">83)</p>

<p class="p1">Reconfiguration does not always do the job. New iron was also added to Estonian</p>

<p class="p1">networks. Preparing for the expected onslaught on late May 8, a 10 Gbps cluster</p>

<p class="p1">was set up to support ICT services for governmental systems. This effort, led and</p>

<p class="p1">implemented by CERT EE, was supported by network equipment manufacturers</p>

<p class="p1">that voluntarily lent their hardware in non-office hours despite the absence of contractual</p>

<p class="p1">agreements or service level agreements that would have required such</p>

<p class="p1">prompt support (Interview 87).</p>

<p class="p1">Eventually, the primary line of defence had to be moved away from the IT departments</p>

<p class="p1">of affected organisations. Filtering inbound traffic at Estonia&rsquo;s upstream</p>

<p class="p1">providers was a more viable path. The basic strategy in a DDoS attack is to stop</p>

<p class="p1">malicious packets already at upstream providers.</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">For such an approach, collaboration with first Estonian and then international</p>

<p class="p1">network providers was inevitable. Upstream providers needed addresses of Estonian</p>

<p class="p1">targets, analysed the traffic going through their networks to target addresses.</p>

<p class="p1">When they see indications of malicious intent, they &ldquo;try to limit that, &hellip;try to clean</p>

<p class="p1">the traffic&rdquo;, or establish a threshold for maximum traffic &ldquo;just to give some breathing</p>

<p class="p1">space&rdquo; for the affected Estonian sites (Interview 65). Estonian ISPs tried to</p>

<p class="p1">create such &ldquo;breathing space&rdquo; partly by blocking any traffic from abroad going to</p>

<p class="p1">web services that were being attacked. While this nurtured the impression among</p>

<p class="p1">foreign observers that an entire country was brought down, Estonian could still</p>

<p class="p1">enjoy services that were mostly directed at them in the first place.</p>

<p class="p1">A more sophisticated technique than bulk-blocking of inbound foreign traffic is to</p>

<p class="p1">find precise patterns by which attacking packets can be distinguished from normal</p>

<p class="p1">packets. Network providers could then identify and drop such packets into whichever</p>

<p class="p1">segment of the Internet between two communicating ends in which they occur.</p>

<p class="p1">Distributing such attack descriptions up to the traffic chain is in the best</p>

<p class="p1">economic interest of all providers, the networks of which are used to conveying</p>

<p class="p1">large amounts of malicious traffic to the target address. Network expert Bill</p>

<p class="p1">Woodcock elaborates:</p>

<p class="p1">&ldquo;But as your service provider, [the bad traffic is] still consuming resources</p>

<p class="p1">in my network, it's coming to me and I throw it away. You are</p>

<p class="p1">not paying any extra for that. So I take that attack description and send</p>

<p class="p1">it to each of my peers [i.e., network providers with which the service</p>

<p class="p1">provider has a peering agreement; AS] who are sending me that traffic</p>

<p class="p1">because they are also not getting paid for the attack traffic, so it's in</p>

<p class="p1">their best interest to filter that traffic. &hellip; It's usually not coming from a</p>

<p class="p1">single source, but from a botnet. But you usually don't go all the way</p>

<p class="p1">back to each source, at that point it's so diffuse that it doesn't make</p>

<p class="p1">sense anymore.&rdquo;</p>

<p class="p1">Identifying the difference between good and bad packets is a clear technological</p>

<p class="p1">challenge.</p>

<p class="p1">&ldquo;If I'm your ISP, I just notice more packets. I can't tell whether you had</p>

<p class="p1">a successful ad campaign or whether this is an attacker. &hellip; You need to</p>

<p class="p1">define the difference between a normal packet and an attack packet.</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">This is where a capable CERT can come to the rescue as they might be in a better</p>

<p class="p1">position to identify these bad packets (Interview 23). Increasingly though, vendors</p>

<p class="p1">of network devices provide solutions that can fulfil this task.</p>

<p class="p1">Heavy DDoS attacks are usually delivered by botnets. The Estonian attacks were</p>

<p class="p1">no different. There were some attempts to understand the botnets involved in the</p>

<p class="p1">attacks. As a strategy against a DDoS attack however, disrupting a botnet is not a</p>

<p class="p1">feasible approach for an ongoing attack. Once a botnet has some degree of malicious</p>

<p class="p1">sophistication, the time span it takes to understand the intricacies of the botnet</p>

<p class="p1">malware, let alone to implement a proper mitigation strategy, is way too long</p>

<p class="p1">to be a viable anti-DDoS approach. Furthermore, anti-botnet research was in its</p>

<p class="p1">infancy in 2007. Continuous tracking of botnets was only started after the Estonian</p>

<p class="p1">incidents by the likes of Shadowserver, Arbor Networks or Team Cymru (Interview</p>

<p class="p1">84). Consequentially, it is not known how many and which botnets were used</p>

<p class="p1">for the attacks (Interview 84).</p>

<p class="p1">While this study focuses on the responses to incidents by communities of technical</p>

<p class="p1">experts, the response also had a political dimension. The activities by politicians</p>

<p class="p1">has had little, if any effect on the effective technical response by the Estonian and</p>

<p class="p1">global Internet security community. The Estonian and Western policy sphere</p>

<p class="p1">mainly responded with finger pointing at Russian authorities and a series of blame</p>

<p class="p1">games and diplomatic arm wrestling. It was not and has never been proved that</p>

<p class="p1">Russian authorities were involved in the attacks, and, if so, at which level. But the</p>

<p class="p1">high-level political response signalled to those who might be considering Internet</p>

<p class="p1">bullying that they would have to face political consequences. Whether this has had</p>

<p class="p1">any impact on those who rented botnets for the DDoS attacks and engaged in</p>

<p class="p1">some targeted attacks is only speculative. Not speculative however, but inevitable,</p>

<p class="p1">was the support of actors outside of Estonia in managing the incoming torrent of</p>

<p class="p1">malicious packets.</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">Before proceeding with the description of events in Estonia in May 2007, a brief</p>

<p class="p1">introduction to CERT communities is required. Computer Emergency Response</p>

<p class="p1">Teams (CERTs) or Computer Security Incident Response Teams (CSIRTs) function</p>

<p class="p1">as operational units to support individuals and organisations to adequately</p>

<p class="p1">respond to a security attack.<span class="s1">79 </span>Such CSIRTs are set up by large corporations or</p>

<p class="p1">political bodies willing to consolidate responses to security incidents, to establish</p>

<p class="p1">support centres for affected organisations or, in the case of corporate CSIRTs,</p>

<p class="p1">organisational units. Different CSIRTs cover different organisations, sectors, regions,</p>

<p class="p1">and countries. Their communicational cultures differ widely. While academic</p>

<p class="p1">CSIRTs are accessible and communicative, staff of military CSIRTs have</p>

<p class="p1">generally shied away from answering non-small-talk questions, let alone taking</p>

<p class="p1">part in research interviews. Many CSIRTs are publicly funded organisations, or</p>

<p class="p1">rather teams in publicly funded organisations.</p>

<p class="p1">CSIRTs themselves build communities via umbrella organisations. The Forum of</p>

<p class="p1">Incident Response and Security Teams (FIRST) is probably the institution with</p>

<p class="p1">the widest geographical reach among them. FIRST serves as a hub for more than</p>

<p class="p1">210 CSIRTs worldwide, hosting an annual conference in different locations</p>

<p class="p1">around the globe that attract several hundreds, if not thousands, of participants</p>

<p class="p1">every year.<span class="s1">80 </span>Next to information exchange by presentations, networking is among</p>

<p class="p1">the key goals of FIRST&rsquo;S annual conference.</p>

<p class="p1">&ldquo;When you do the organization for an event like this, you specifically</p>

<p class="p1">look for hotels that have a lot of chairs, where 2, 3, or 4 people can sit</p>

<p class="p1">together and work on a problem.&rdquo; (Interview 43)</p>

<p class="p1">Security communities are not purely virtual communities, they also rely on personal</p>

<p class="p1">meetings.</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">Already in the 1990s, there were plans to establish a dedicated European CERT,</p>

<p class="p1">code-named EUROCERT. However, interests of European states proved to be</p>

<p class="p1">too diverse to pool their response activities in one continent-wide body. Despite</p>

<p class="p1">the loud fanfares from Brussels politicians, the recently established EU-CERT</p>

<p class="p1">merely serves as the response team for the EU bureaucracy. After the EUROCERT</p>

<p class="p1">plans ran aground, the Amsterdam-based Trans-European Research and</p>

<p class="p1">Education Networking Association (TERENA) set up a CSIRT task force (TFCSIRT).</p>

<p class="p1"><span class="s1">81 </span>Until today, TERENA and its TF-CSIRT serve as a mentor for the</p>

<p class="p1">establishment of new CSIRTs and as a hub for CSIRTs to establish collaborative</p>

<p class="p1">networks with their European colleagues. Its Trusted Introducer programme liaises</p>

<p class="p1">new CSIRTs and their staff to the established CSIRTs, warrants the trustworthiness</p>

<p class="p1">of a CSIRT with its accreditation programme. The frequent TF-CSIRT</p>

<p class="p1">meetings create networking opportunities. In general, the purpose of TF-CSIRT</p>

<p class="p1">has been to facilitate networking among European response teams that allow them</p>

<p class="p1">to exchange ideas, information and issue requests for assistance in non-binding</p>

<p class="p1">ways. Within this network of European CSIRTs, governmental CERTs gather in</p>

<p class="p1">their very own &ldquo;close-knit group&rdquo; (Interview 82). Likewise, CERTs with similar</p>

<p class="p1">constituencies tend to have their own communities (Interview 14).</p>

<p class="p1">By early May 2007, the Estonian security experts had not yet developed deep trust</p>

<p class="p1">relationships with global security communities, which so frequently dealt with</p>

<p class="p1">large-scale DDoS attacks on the Internet. This omission required a belated correction</p>

<p class="p1">given the necessity for global collaboration, which translates to the exchange</p>

<p class="p1">of potentially delicate data and the cutting off pf users in the domain of Internet</p>

<p class="p1">security. The Estonians first created a bridge to their peers in European CERTs.</p>

<p class="p1">The head of CERT EE flew to Prague to attend the 21st TF-CSIRT meeting in</p>

<p class="p1">Prague on &mdash; lucky timing &mdash; May 3-4 to raise the attention of the European</p>

<p class="p1">CERT community.<span class="s1">82 </span>Aarelaid gave a brief presentation at the closed Trusted Introducer</p>

<p class="p1">meeting on the first day about what was going on in Estonia and how the</p>

<p class="p1">European CERT community could support the Estonians.<span class="s1">83 </span>Trusted Introducer is</p>

<p class="p1">a programme of TF-CSIRT to accredit CERTs and CSIRTs. CERT EE, which&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">was in operation only for a good year, had not yet run through all the steps required</p>

<p class="p1">to get accredited. But given the circumstances, Aarelaid could attend the</p>

<p class="p1">closed session and give an account of the incident, its effects and likely causes (Interview</p>

<p class="p1">14). Even if participants had no direct control over network devices,</p>

<p class="p1">CERTs&rsquo; staff usually have close connections to upstream providers or regular ISPs,</p>

<p class="p1">both of which are helpful in an anti-DDoS response.</p>

<p class="p1">Lucky conference timing came to the rescue a second time. The cooperation between</p>

<p class="p1">the international and the Estonian communities was significantly eased by a</p>

<p class="p1">long-planned RIPE meeting in Tallinn on May 7.<span class="s1">84 </span>It was during this RIPE meeting</p>

<p class="p1">that members of the Estonian technical community were eventually introduced</p>

<p class="p1">to members of the global Internet security community and gauged as trustworthy.</p>

<p class="p1">However, for this to happen, many gallons of kerosene had to be burned. In January</p>

<p class="p1">2007, Aarelaid flew to Seattle to attend a closed meeting of &ldquo;one of those security</p>

<p class="p1">operational trust groups&rdquo; hosted by Microsoft in January 2007 (Interview 69).</p>

<p class="p1">One of the anti-botnet vanguards apparently paved the way for him to get to that</p>

<p class="p1">meeting:</p>

<p class="p1">&ldquo;One of the conferences they [the Internet Security Operations community]</p>

<p class="p1">ran was hosted by Microsoft. Hillar sent me a message that he</p>

<p class="p1">wanted to come, an email. And for me, Estonia was this place in Eastern</p>

<p class="p1">Europe where all this cybercrime was coming from. And I didn't</p>

<p class="p1">know what to do with it. I thought it was a joke. Some cybercriminal</p>

<p class="p1">trying to come to the conference. But I decided to take a risk and I</p>

<p class="p1">brought Hillar there.&rdquo; (Interview 15)</p>

<p class="p1">At this meeting in Seattle, Aarelaid met a person that would happen to attend the</p>

<p class="p1">RIPE meeting in Tallinn and eventually be decisive in closing the gap in the networks</p>

<p class="p1">of security experts.</p>

<p class="p1">&ldquo;He actually called me and said that [he wanted to ask me for] a favour.&hellip;</p>

<p class="p1">[whether I could] do introductions for him because he hadn't</p>

<p class="p1">yet been formally vetted into an operational security group. &hellip; [H]e was</p>

<p class="p1">trying, he was in the midst of getting vetted, but he wasn't yet. &hellip; And</p>

<p class="p1">so I made the introductions&hellip;.&rdquo;</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">This &ldquo;trusted intermediary&rdquo; introduced Aarelaid to two experts who had key roles</p>

<p class="p1">in the community of network operations, and long-standing international industry</p>

<p class="p1">experience. With access to these persons, the Estonian community gained support</p>

<p class="p1">for Estonia&rsquo;s important upstream providers and could move the packet blockade</p>

<p class="p1">further away from their systems (Interview 69; Interview 23; Interview 65). The</p>

<p class="p1">RIPE meeting on Monday, May 7, was both the perfect and the last opportunity</p>

<p class="p1">to bridge this gap in the informal response networks in an informal environment.</p>

<p class="p1">With this newly achieved status as members of the technical community, a few</p>

<p class="p1">Estonians could now send requests or lists with attacking IP addresses to mailinglist-</p>

<p class="p1">based security communities such as NSP-Sec.<span class="s1">85 </span>Network security professionals</p>

<p class="p1">around the world that are members of such a list would then help to stop malicious</p>

<p class="p1">traffic from flowing from their networks towards Estonian systems.</p>

<p class="p1">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">Other security services</p>

<p class="p2">The production of Internet security in the Estonian case consisted of a successful</p>

<p class="p2">mitigation of a mix of DDoS attacks, web defacements, and other malicious hacking</p>

<p class="p2">attempts. This overall service can best be separated into five distinct services</p>

<p class="p2">delivered by various Estonian and international actors. Situational awareness was</p>

<p class="p2">established by the monitoring of technical and social networks. DDoS mitigation</p>

<p class="p2">helped to minimize the impact of the main attack technique used against Estonian</p>

<p class="p2">systems. These were the two main components of the Estonian response. Others</p>

<p class="p2">included policing, malware analysis, and the somewhat vague category of information</p>

<p class="p2">sharing and distribution. The main technical defence approach was presented</p>

<p class="p2">earlier in section 5.1.3. This section discusses situational awareness,</p>

<p class="p2">distribution of information, policing, and malware analysis.</p>

<p class="p2">Given the state of technology in spring 2007, responding to DDoS attacks on several</p>

<p class="p2">organisations required data sharing among these organisations or their ICT</p>

<p class="p2">providers. In Estonia, no central monitoring system existed that would have kept</p>

<p class="p2">an eye on the health of Estonian web-based services. Individual organisations</p>

<p class="p2">monitored their systems, security and AV service providers their clients&rsquo; systems,</p>

<p class="p2">but the countrywide state was not directly displayed on the monitors of some security</p>

<p class="p2">professionals in Estonia. But there were workarounds. The IRC rooms used by</p>

<p class="p2">the Estonian security community served as an improvised monitoring system that</p>

<p class="p2">displayed problems with certain segments of the network. When two or more</p>

<p class="p2">members from one organisation dropped out at the same time, then their networks</p>

<p class="p2">likely had an issue (Interview 87). Most importantly however, security experts</p>

<p class="p2">shared their knowledge with their peers in the Estonian community, achieving</p>

<p class="p2">sufficient situational knowledge in the absence of central monitoring. As with</p>

<p class="p2">DDoS mitigation, larger Estonian organisations and especially ISPs had their network</p>

<p class="p2">monitoring systems in place (Interview 82). Their picture was complemented</p>

<p class="p2">by the global community, where major ISPs with &ldquo;global outreach have a very good</p>

<p class="p2">visibility on DDoS activity.&rdquo; (Interview 82) When the attacks started and new</p>

<p class="p2">events occurred during the almost-four-week period, peers in the response com&nbsp;</p>

<p class="p1">munity were briefed rapidly. At some point, the response community agreed on</p>

<p class="p1">cancelling their established reporting mechanism, which required any organisation</p>

<p class="p1">to formally report an incident to the national CERT (Interview 87). The absence</p>

<p class="p1">of written documents is certainly detrimental for historians and public managers</p>

<p class="p1">who want to give an account of past events or review the performance of their Internet</p>

<p class="p1">security institutions.</p>

<p class="p1">A second aspect of situational awareness refers to a knowledge of the activities of</p>

<p class="p1">potential perpetrators. Russian underground fora are monitored by commercial</p>

<p class="p1">security service providers, individual researchers, and presumably some governmental</p>

<p class="p1">agencies as well. These fora are places where hackers, carders, botherders, malware</p>

<p class="p1">authors and persons with other roles in the digital crime scene convene and</p>

<p class="p1">collaborate (Interview 73).</p>

<p class="p1">&ldquo;Dozens, if not hundreds &mdash; every single underground forum is monitored</p>

<p class="p1">by a big number of people data-mining.&rdquo; (Interview 73)</p>

<p class="p1">Information about potential backers of the Estonian attacks was shared within the</p>

<p class="p1">Estonian community.</p>

<p class="p1">Furthermore, security teams and CERTs also need to stay on top of the news</p>

<p class="p1">stream on the latest current security issues. They keep an eye on a substantial number</p>

<p class="p1">of news sources and observe various sources that collect &ldquo;badness information&rdquo;<span class="s1">87</span></p>

<p class="p1">like Clean mx, Spam Cops, Spamhouse, T-Shield, Abuse Helper, and other trackers.</p>

<p class="p1">A national CERT can normalize and sanitize such information and send it to</p>

<p class="p1">their constituency.</p>

<p class="p1">Conveying lists of attacking systems from the attackee to networks closer to the</p>

<p class="p1">attackers has been an important element in the Estonian response. Within Estonia,</p>

<p class="p1">the communication channels of the local security communities were used. For</p>

<p class="p1">trans-border communication, CERT communities or mailing lists like NSP-Sec</p>

<p class="p1">helped to convey information. ISPs were also contacted directly or via their respective</p>

<p class="p1">national CERT (Interview 82). National CERTs often act as intermediaries to</p>

<p class="p1">convey or receive information on behalf of their constituency, especially when affected</p>

<p class="p1">organisations do not have direct access to these communities via an employee-</p>

<p class="p1">member. In the Estonian case, the Finnish CERT FI supported their Estonian</p>

<p class="p1">counterpart by preparing lists for the international community. Estonian organisations</p>

<p class="p1">send lists of suspicious IP addresses to CERT EE, which then &ldquo;semi-</p>

<p class="p1">automatically produced this lists of attackers&rdquo; (Interview 87). This consolidated list</p>

<p class="p1">was then sent to CERT FI, which took care of international distribution (Interview</p>

<p class="p1">87). Eventually, the CERT FI was attacked, too, and busy defending itself, so</p>

<p class="p1">that the Estonian CERT then lost its most staunch supporter. Luckily, the attacks</p>

<p class="p1">had already passed their nadir, so it &ldquo;was not a big problem&rdquo; (Interview 87). CERT</p>

<p class="p1">EE took CERT FI&rsquo;s automation scripts and managed to accomplish the task of</p>

<p class="p1">information distribution itself.</p>

<p class="p1">A fundamental aspect of security production in general is policing, an aspect that</p>

<p class="p1">had been ignored by technical Internet security communities in very early years. In</p>

<p class="p1">constitutional states, sanctioning actors for malevolent activities is transferred to</p>

<p class="p1">the state&rsquo;s legal system. Beyond and besides, social systems usually have their own</p>

<p class="p1">mechanisms to sanction activities that are unwanted by parts of the population.</p>

<p class="p1">Policing and sanctioning on the Internet has been made somewhat difficult by the</p>

<p class="p1">attribution problem. Proving that an unlawful activity has been committed by a</p>

<p class="p1">certain person is difficult when a perpetrator applies sophisticated anonymisation</p>

<p class="p1">and camouflage techniques. Estonian police arrested an Estonian schoolboy who</p>

<p class="p1">committed some very basic manual attacks, but actually only played a marginal</p>

<p class="p1">role. Nonetheless, he was the only one arrested for involvement in the Estonian</p>

<p class="p1">incident. Requests for investigative assistance to Russian authorities bore no fruits.</p>
